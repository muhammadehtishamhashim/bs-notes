# Linear Diophantine Equation

A **Diophantine Equation** is a polynomial equation with two or more integer unknowns. 

A **Linear Diophantine Equation (LDE)** restricts the integer unknowns to a maximum degree of $1$. In two variables, it takes the standard form:

<MathWrapper title="Standard Form">
$$ ax + by = c $$
</MathWrapper>

Where $a, b,$ and $c$ are integer constants, and $x, y$ are the unknown integer variables.

---

## Homogeneous Linear Diophantine Equation (HLDE)

An HLDE is a special case where $c = 0$, taking the form:
$$ ax + by = 0 $$

<Callout title="Trivial Solution" type="info">
Note that $x = 0$ and $y = 0$ is always a solution. This is known as the **trivial solution**. Furthermore, because it is an addition equating to zero, at least one of the variables must be negative for any non-trivial solution.
</Callout>

### Theorem: Solving HLDE
If $\text{GCD}(a,b) = d$, then the complete family of solutions to $ax + by = 0$ is:
$$ x = k\left(\frac{b}{d}\right) \quad \text{and} \quad y = -k\left(\frac{a}{d}\right) \quad \text{for any } k \in \mathbb{Z} $$

## Exam Critical Points
<div className="bg-red-500/5 border-l-4 border-red-500 p-4 rounded-r-xl my-6">
  <ul className="list-disc pl-5 space-y-2 text-sm font-medium text-foreground">
    <li><strong className="text-red-500">The Existence Rule:</strong> A linear Diophantine equation $ax + by = c$ has an integer solution <em>if and only if</em> $\text{GCD}(a, b)$ perfectly divides $c$. If it does not, you must state "No integer solution exists."</li>
    <li><strong className="text-red-500">Trivial Solutions:</strong> The equation $ax + by = 0$ will ALWAYS have the trivial solution $(0, 0)$.</li>
    <li>When finding general solutions, remember to express your final answer in terms of an arbitrary integer constant $k$ (e.g., $x = 3k - 4$, $y = -5k + 8$).</li>
  </ul>
</div>

## Comprehensive Summary
* **What it is:** An algebraic equation targeting multiple variables where solutions are strictly restricted to integers.
* **Core Mechanism:** You determine if a solution exists using the GCD of the coefficients. If it does, you find one basic "Particular Solution," convert the problem to equal 0 (Homogeneous), and solve it to find an infinite family of possible valid coordinates.
* **Significance:** Used heavily in cryptography to find inverse elements and solve modular equations by reformulating $ax \equiv 1 \pmod m$ into the LDE $ax - my = 1$.
