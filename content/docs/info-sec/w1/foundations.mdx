# Introduction to Information Security

**Information Security (InfoSec)** is the practice of protecting physical and electronic information from unauthorized access, use, disclosure, disruption, modification, or destruction. It requires a multi-disciplinary approach involving people, processes, and technology.

<Callout title="Historical Context" type="info">
The need to secure information dates back centuries. During WWI, multi-tier classification systems were developed for sensitive information. A famous milestone is **Alan Turing's** successful decryption of the German Enigma Machine during WWII, heavily influencing modern cryptography.
</Callout>

### Extended Principles
Beyond the CIA Triad, robust InfoSec programs enforce three additional principles:
* **Non-repudiation:** Ensures a party cannot deny sending or receiving a message/transaction (requires Data Integrity and Authenticity).
* **Authenticity:** Verifying that users/systems are exactly who they claim to be, and inputs are from trusted sources.
* **Accountability:** The ability to trace actions on a system uniquely back to the specific entity that performed them.

## Governance & Compliance

To manage risks at an organizational scale, frameworks and laws are established.

* **ISMS (Information Security Management System):** A structured framework (like ISO/IEC 27001) that includes policies, procedures, and controls to continuously manage and secure sensitive data from cyber threats.
* **GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation):** An EU privacy law (effective May 2018) granting individuals control over their personal data. It mandates strict rules on data collection, transparency, and enforces significant fines for non-compliance.

## Modern InfoSec Challenges

The landscape of information security is continuously evolving, facing threats such as:

1. **Cyber Threats:** Escalating sophistication of malware, ransomware, and phishing attacks.
2. **The Human Element:** Insider threats (malicious employees) and human error (weak passwords, clicking malicious links).
3. **Hardware Sprawl:** The explosion of easily lost/stolen Mobile and IoT devices with natively weak security controls.
4. **Third-Party Integrations:** Connecting internal systems to external vendors introduces supply-chain vulnerabilities.
5. **Globalization & Privacy:** Navigating conflicting data storage/processing laws across international borders while maintaining strict data privacy.
