# Congruence

In number theory and cryptography, **Congruence** is a way of saying that two numbers have the same remainder when divided by a specific number (called the **modulus**). It is the foundational concept behind modular arithmetic, often called "clock arithmetic."

### The Mathematical Definition

Two integers $a$ and $b$ are said to be **congruent modulo $n$** if their difference $(a - b)$ is an integer multiple of $n$. 

This means $n$ perfectly divides $(a - b)$, written mathematically as $n \mid (a - b)$. 

<MathWrapper title="Congruence Notation">
$$ a \equiv b \pmod n $$
</MathWrapper>

**Example:**
* $38 \equiv 14 \pmod{12}$ because $38 - 14 = 24$, and $12$ perfectly divides $24$.
* Conceptually, if the time is 14:00 (2 PM), adding 24 hours makes it 38:00, which on a 12-hour clock is still exactly the same time (2 PM).

Modular arithmetic behaves very similarly to normal algebra, making it incredibly powerful for encryption algorithms.

## Modular Inverse

In normal arithmetic, the inverse of $5$ is $\frac{1}{5}$, because $5 \times \frac{1}{5} = 1$. 

In modular arithmetic, we deal only with integers. The **Modular Multiplicative Inverse** of $a$ modulo $m$ is an integer $x$ such that:

<MathWrapper title="Modular Inverse">
$$ a \cdot x \equiv 1 \pmod m $$
</MathWrapper>

We often write this inverse as $a^{-1}$.

<Callout title="Condition for Existence" type="warn">
A modular inverse $a^{-1} \pmod m$ **only exists** if $a$ and $m$ are coprime, meaning $\text{GCD}(a, m) = 1$. If they share factors, the inverse does not exist.
</Callout>

### Finding the Inverse
**Example:** Find the inverse of $3$ modulo $7$. (i.e., solve $3x \equiv 1 \pmod 7$)
* Try $x = 1$: $3(1) = 3 \equiv 3 \pmod 7$
* Try $x = 2$: $3(2) = 6 \equiv 6 \pmod 7$
* Try $x = 3$: $3(3) = 9 \equiv 2 \pmod 7$
* Try $x = 4$: $3(4) = 12 \equiv 5 \pmod 7$
* Try $x = 5$: $3(5) = 15 \equiv 1 \pmod 7$ **(Match!)**

Therefore, $5$ is the modular inverse of $3$ under modulo $7$. We can write $3^{-1} \equiv 5 \pmod 7$.

*(Note: Finding modular inverses is a critical step for executing the Chinese Remainder Theorem in the next module).*
