# Chinese Remainder Theorem (CRT)

The **Chinese Remainder Theorem (CRT)** is a powerful mathematical theorem used to find a unique solution to a set of different congruent equations with one variable ($X$), provided that their moduli are relatively prime (coprime) to each other.

### The Problem Structure

You are given a system of equations in the form:
$$ X \equiv a_1 \pmod{m_1} $$
$$ X \equiv a_2 \pmod{m_2} $$
$$ \dots $$
$$ X \equiv a_n \pmod{m_n} $$

*CRT states that this system has a unique solution modulo $M$ (where $M$ is the product of all individual moduli) IF and ONLY IF the moduli ($m_1, m_2, \dots, m_n$) are relatively prime pairwise.*

<MathWrapper title="The Master CRT Formula">
$$ X = (a_1 M_1 M_1^{-1} + a_2 M_2 M_2^{-1} + \dots + a_n M_n M_n^{-1}) \pmod M $$
</MathWrapper>

## Applied Examples

### Example 1: Three Equations

Solve the following system for $X$:
1. $X \equiv 2 \pmod 3$
2. $X \equiv 3 \pmod 5$
3. $X \equiv 2 \pmod 7$

**Extracted Variables:**
* $a_1 = 2, m_1 = 3$
* $a_2 = 3, m_2 = 5$
* $a_3 = 2, m_3 = 7$

#### Step 1: Find $M$
$M = m_1 \times m_2 \times m_3 = 3 \times 5 \times 7 = \mathbf{105}$

#### Step 2: Find Partial Moduli ($M_i$)
* $M_1 = \frac{M}{m_1} = \frac{105}{3} = \mathbf{35}$
* $M_2 = \frac{M}{m_2} = \frac{105}{5} = \mathbf{21}$
* $M_3 = \frac{M}{m_3} = \frac{105}{7} = \mathbf{15}$

#### Step 3: Find Inverses ($M_i^{-1}$)

* **For $M_1^{-1}$:**
  $35 \times M_1^{-1} \equiv 1 \pmod 3$
  *(Since $35 \equiv 2 \pmod 3$)*
  $2 \times M_1^{-1} \equiv 1 \pmod 3 \implies \mathbf{M_1^{-1} = 2}$ (Because $2 \times 2 = 4 \equiv 1 \pmod 3$).

* **For $M_2^{-1}$:**
  $21 \times M_2^{-1} \equiv 1 \pmod 5$
  *(Since $21 \equiv 1 \pmod 5$)*
  $1 \times M_2^{-1} \equiv 1 \pmod 5 \implies \mathbf{M_2^{-1} = 1}$.

* **For $M_3^{-1}$:**
  $15 \times M_3^{-1} \equiv 1 \pmod 7$
  *(Since $15 \equiv 1 \pmod 7$)*
  $1 \times M_3^{-1} \equiv 1 \pmod 7 \implies \mathbf{M_3^{-1} = 1}$.

#### Step 4: Master Formula

<MathWrapper title="Final Calculation">
$$
\begin{aligned}
X &= (a_1 M_1 M_1^{-1} + a_2 M_2 M_2^{-1} + a_3 M_3 M_3^{-1}) \pmod M \\[1em]
X &= (2 \times 35 \times 2 + 3 \times 21 \times 1 + 2 \times 15 \times 1) \pmod{105} \\[1em]
X &= (140 + 63 + 30) \pmod{105} \\[1em]
X &= 233 \pmod{105}
\end{aligned}
$$
</MathWrapper>

To reduce $233 \pmod{105}$, we subtract $105$ twice ($210$). 
$X = 233 - 210 = \mathbf{23}$

**Final Answer: $X = 23$** *(Check: $23 \pmod 3 = 2$, $23 \pmod 5 = 3$, $23 \pmod 7 = 2$. It works!)*

### Example 2: Two Equations

Solve the following system for $X$:
1. $X \equiv 1 \pmod 4$
2. $X \equiv 3 \pmod 5$

**Extracted Variables:**
* $a_1 = 1, m_1 = 4$
* $a_2 = 3, m_2 = 5$

#### Process:
1. **$M$:** $M = 4 \times 5 = \mathbf{20}$
2. **$M_i$:** * $M_1 = \frac{20}{4} = \mathbf{5}$
   * $M_2 = \frac{20}{5} = \mathbf{4}$
3. **Inverses ($M_i^{-1}$):**
   * $5 \times M_1^{-1} \equiv 1 \pmod 4 \implies 1 \times M_1^{-1} \equiv 1 \pmod 4 \implies \mathbf{M_1^{-1} = 1}$
   * $4 \times M_2^{-1} \equiv 1 \pmod 5 \implies \mathbf{M_2^{-1} = 4}$ *(Since $4 \times 4 = 16 \equiv 1 \pmod 5$)*
4. **Formula:**
   $X = (1 \times 5 \times 1 + 3 \times 4 \times 4) \pmod{20}$
   $X = (5 + 48) \pmod{20}$
   $X = 53 \pmod{20}$

Subtract $20$ twice ($40$) to reduce the modulo:
$X = 53 - 40 = \mathbf{13}$

**Final Answer: $X = 13$**
