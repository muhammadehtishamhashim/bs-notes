# Algebra of Matrices

Matrix algebra, as defined in Chapter 3 of the Grade 11 textbook, outlines the formal rules for mathematical operations on matrices.

1. **Equality of Matrices**: Two matrices $A = [a_{ij}]$ and $B = [b_{ij}]$ are strictly equal if they share the exact same order AND their corresponding elements are identical ($a_{ij} = b_{ij}$).
2. **Matrix Addition/Subtraction**: $A \pm B$ is only defined if $A$ and $B$ are of the **same order** (comparable). The result is obtained by adding/subtracting corresponding elements.
3. **Scalar Multiplication**: If matrix $A$ is multiplied by a real number $k$ (a scalar), every single element inside $A$ is multiplied by $k$.
4. **Matrix Multiplication ($AB$)**: The product $AB$ is defined **only if** the number of columns in $A$ equals the number of rows in $B$. The result is calculated using the **Row-by-Column** dot product method.

## Conceptual Questions

### Question 1
What is the fundamental difference in the necessary conditions required to add two matrices versus multiplying two matrices?

**Solution:**
To add two matrices, they must have the exact same order (comparable). To multiply two matrices $A$ and $B$, they do not need the same order; instead, they must be conformable, meaning the number of columns in matrix $A$ must exactly equal the number of rows in matrix $B$.

### Question 2
If matrix $A$ has an order of $m \times n$ and matrix $B$ has an order of $n \times p$, what will be the order of the resulting product matrix $AB$?

**Solution:**
The inner dimensions ($n$) cancel out during the dot product operation. The resulting product matrix $AB$ inherits the number of rows from the first matrix and the number of columns from the second matrix. 

Thus, its order will be $m \times p$.

### Question 3
Explain why matrix multiplication is not commutative using a dimension argument.

**Solution:**
Let $A$ be a $2 \times 3$ matrix and $B$ be a $3 \times 4$ matrix. The product $AB$ is perfectly valid because the inner dimensions match ($3=3$), resulting in a $2 \times 4$ matrix. However, if we attempt $BA$, we are multiplying a $3 \times 4$ matrix by a $2 \times 3$ matrix. The inner dimensions do not match ($4 \neq 2$), meaning the product $BA$ is completely undefined. Therefore, $AB \neq BA$.

## Exercise Questions

### Question 1
Find the values of $a, b, c, d$ given the matrix equality equation below.
$$\begin{bmatrix} a - b & 2a + c \\ 2a - b & 3c + d \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 5 \\ 0 & 13 \end{bmatrix}$$

By the definition of equality, corresponding elements in matching positions must be equal. We set up a system of linear equations and solve them progressively.

<MathWrapper title="System of Equations">

$$
\begin{aligned}
(i)\quad a - b &= -1 \\
(ii)\quad 2a - b &= 0 \\
(iii)\quad 2a + c &= 5 \\
(iv)\quad 3c + d &= 13 \\
b &= 2a \quad \text{(from ii)} \\
a - (2a) &= -1 \implies -a = -1 \implies a = 1 \\
b &= 2(1) = 2 \\
2(1) + c &= 5 \implies c = 3 \\
3(3) + d &= 13 \implies 9 + d = 13 \implies d = 4
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

### Question 2
Given matrices $A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 3 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$ and $B = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ -2 & 5 \end{bmatrix}$, find $A + B$.

Since both matrices are $2 \times 2$, addition is possible. We add the corresponding elements together.

<MathWrapper title="Addition Steps">

$$
\begin{aligned}
A + B &= \begin{bmatrix} 2+1 & 4+3 \\ 3+(-2) & 2+5 \end{bmatrix} \\
A + B &= \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 7 \\ 1 & 7 \end{bmatrix}
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

### Question 3
Find $3A - 2B$ given $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$ and $B = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 4 \\ -2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$.

First, we perform scalar multiplication by multiplying every element in $A$ by 3 and every element in $B$ by 2. Then, we subtract the resulting matrices.

<MathWrapper title="Scalar Multiplication and Subtraction">

$$
\begin{aligned}
3A &= \begin{bmatrix} 3(1) & 3(-1) \\ 3(2) & 3(3) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & -3 \\ 6 & 9 \end{bmatrix} \\
2B &= \begin{bmatrix} 2(0) & 2(4) \\ 2(-2) & 2(1) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 8 \\ -4 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \\
3A - 2B &= \begin{bmatrix} 3 - 0 & -3 - 8 \\ 6 - (-4) & 9 - 2 \end{bmatrix} \\
3A - 2B &= \begin{bmatrix} 3 & -11 \\ 10 & 7 \end{bmatrix}
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

### Question 4
Find matrix $X$ if $2X + \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 8 \\ 7 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$.

Treat the matrices algebraically. Isolate $2X$ by subtracting the constant matrix from both sides, then multiply by the scalar $1/2$.

<MathWrapper title="Solving the Matrix Equation">

$$
\begin{aligned}
2X &= \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 8 \\ 7 & 0 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \\
2X &= \begin{bmatrix} 5-1 & 8-2 \\ 7-3 & 0-4 \end{bmatrix} \\
2X &= \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 6 \\ 4 & -4 \end{bmatrix} \\
X &= \frac{1}{2} \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 6 \\ 4 & -4 \end{bmatrix} \\
X &= \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 2 & -2 \end{bmatrix}
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

### Question 5
Find the product $AB$ if $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$ and $B = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$.

Both matrices are $2 \times 2$. We use the row-by-column dot product method.

<MathWrapper title="Matrix Multiplication">

$$
\begin{aligned}
AB &= \begin{bmatrix} (1)(-1) + (2)(2) & (1)(0) + (2)(1) \\ (3)(-1) + (4)(2) & (3)(0) + (4)(1) \end{bmatrix} \\
AB &= \begin{bmatrix} -1 + 4 & 0 + 2 \\ -3 + 8 & 0 + 4 \end{bmatrix} \\
AB &= \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 2 \\ 5 & 4 \end{bmatrix}
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

### Question 6
Compute $AB$ where $A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ 3 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$ and $B = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 4 & 2 \\ 3 & 1 & 5 \end{bmatrix}$.

Matrix $A$ is $2 \times 2$ and Matrix $B$ is $2 \times 3$. The columns of $A$ match the rows of $B$, so multiplication is conformable. The product will be $2 \times 3$.

<MathWrapper title="Matrix Multiplication">

$$
\begin{aligned}
AB &= \begin{bmatrix} 
(2)(1) + (-1)(3) & (2)(4) + (-1)(1) & (2)(2) + (-1)(5) \\
(3)(1) + (0)(3) & (3)(4) + (0)(1) & (3)(2) + (0)(5) 
\end{bmatrix} \\
AB &= \begin{bmatrix} 2 - 3 & 8 - 1 & 4 - 5 \\ 3 + 0 & 12 + 0 & 6 + 0 \end{bmatrix} \\
AB &= \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 7 & -1 \\ 3 & 12 & 6 \end{bmatrix}
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

### Question 7
Let $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$ and $B = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$. Show that $AB \neq BA$.

We will compute the product $AB$ and then independently compute the product $BA$ to observe the differences in the resulting matrices.

<MathWrapper title="Commutativity Test">

$$
\begin{aligned}
AB &= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \\
BA &= \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

Since corresponding elements differ (e.g., $1 \neq -1$), we prove that $AB \neq BA$.

### Question 8
Evaluate $\begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 2 \\ -5 \end{bmatrix}$.

We are multiplying a $1 \times 3$ row vector by a $3 \times 1$ column vector. The resulting matrix will have an order of $1 \times 1$.

<MathWrapper title="Vector Multiplication">

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{Product} &= \begin{bmatrix} (2)(4) + (-1)(2) + (3)(-5) \end{bmatrix} \\
&= \begin{bmatrix} 8 - 2 - 15 \end{bmatrix} \\
&= \begin{bmatrix} -9 \end{bmatrix}
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

### Question 9
Verify the distributive property $A(B + C) = AB + AC$ given $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$, $B = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$, $C = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 2 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$.

We will evaluate the Left Hand Side (LHS) by adding first, and the Right Hand Side (RHS) by multiplying first.

<MathWrapper title="Distributive Property Check">

$$
\begin{aligned}
B + C &= \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 3 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \\
\text{LHS} &= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 3 & 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3+6 & 1+6 \\ 0+3 & 0+3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 9 & 7 \\ 3 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \\
AB &= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 6 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \\
AC &= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 2 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 1 \\ 2 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \\
\text{RHS} &= AB + AC = \begin{bmatrix} 4+5 & 6+1 \\ 1+2 & 3+0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 9 & 7 \\ 3 & 3 \end{bmatrix}
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

Since LHS equals RHS, the property is verified.

### Question 10
Evaluate the matrix polynomial $f(A) = A^2 - 4A + 5I_2$ where $A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$.

We break the polynomial into three distinct calculations: squaring matrix $A$, performing scalar multiplication on $A$, scaling the identity matrix, and then combining the results.

<MathWrapper title="Polynomial Evaluation">

$$
\begin{aligned}
A^2 &= \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4-1 & -2-2 \\ 2+2 & -1+4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & -4 \\ 4 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \\
4A &= \begin{bmatrix} 8 & -4 \\ 4 & 8 \end{bmatrix} \\
5I_2 &= \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 0 \\ 0 & 5 \end{bmatrix} \\
f(A) &= \begin{bmatrix} 3 & -4 \\ 4 & 3 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 8 & -4 \\ 4 & 8 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 0 \\ 0 & 5 \end{bmatrix} \\
f(A) &= \begin{bmatrix} 3 - 8 + 5 & -4 - (-4) + 0 \\ 4 - 4 + 0 & 3 - 8 + 5 \end{bmatrix} \\
f(A) &= \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>
