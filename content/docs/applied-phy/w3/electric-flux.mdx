# What is Electric Flux ($\Phi$)?

**Electric Flux** ($\Phi$, pronounced "phi") is a measure of the total number of electric field lines passing through a specific surface area. It helps us quantify how much of an electric field is penetrating a given boundary.

To calculate flux, we use the dot product of the Electric Field vector ($\vec{E}$) and the Area vector ($\vec{A}$).

<MathWrapper title="Electric Flux Formula">

$$
\Phi = \vec{E} \cdot \vec{A} \\[1em]
\Phi = E A \cos\theta
$$

</MathWrapper>

### Understanding the Area Vector ($\vec{A}$)
A crucial concept here is the **Area Vector**. The direction of an area vector is always **perpendicular (normal) to the surface**. 
* If you place a book flat on a table, its surface area vector points straight up toward the ceiling.
* The angle $\theta$ in our formula is the angle between the electric field lines and this perpendicular area vector.

# Calculating Flux: Three Orientations

Depending on how a surface is oriented in an electric field, the flux changes based on the angle $\theta$. Let's look at the three main cases.

### Case I: Maximum Positive Flux ($\theta = 0^\circ$)
The surface is exactly perpendicular to the electric field. The field lines hit it head-on, in the exact same direction as the Area Vector.

<MathWrapper title="Maximum Positive Flux">

$$
\begin{aligned}
\Phi &= EA \cos(0^\circ) \\
\Phi &= EA (1) \\
\Phi &= +EA \quad \text{(Maximum Outward Flux)}
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

### Case II: Zero Flux ($\theta = 90^\circ$)
The surface is flat/parallel to the electric field. The lines simply glide over the surface without piercing it. The Area Vector is completely perpendicular ($90^\circ$) to the field lines.

<MathWrapper title="Zero Flux">

$$
\begin{aligned}
\Phi &= EA \cos(90^\circ) \\
\Phi &= EA (0) \\
\Phi &= 0 \quad \text{(Minimum Magnitude)}
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

### Case III: Minimum Flux ($\theta = 180^\circ$)
The surface is perpendicular to the field, but the field lines are travelling in the exact *opposite* direction of the Area Vector (e.g., lines entering a closed box instead of leaving it). 

<MathWrapper title="Maximum Negative Flux">

$$
\begin{aligned}
\Phi &= EA \cos(180^\circ) \\
\Phi &= EA (-1) \\
\Phi &= -EA \quad \text{(Algebraic Minimum / Inward Flux)}
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

# Flux through Variable Surfaces (Calculus Approach)

To calculate flux for complex or curved shapes, we break the large Area ($A$) into infinitely small area elements ($d\vec{A}$). We calculate the tiny amount of flux ($d\Phi$) through one element, and then **integrate** over the entire surface.

<MathWrapper title="Integral Form of Flux">

$$
\begin{aligned}
d\Phi &= \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} \\[1em]
\Phi &= \int \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A}
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

<Callout title="Correction from Class Notes" type="warn">
In the handwritten notes, the derivation for the SI unit mistakenly used "$Q = EA$". $Q$ stands for Charge. The correct equation is $\Phi = EA$. Therefore, the SI unit of Electric Flux is calculated as Force/Charge $\times$ Area: $\frac{\text{Newtons}}{\text{Coulomb}} \times \text{Meters}^2$, resulting in **$Nm^2/C$**.
</Callout>

## Comprehensive Summary

* **Definition:** Electric flux ($\Phi$) measures how many electric field lines pierce through a specific area.
* **Formula:** $\Phi = \vec{E} \cdot \vec{A} = EA\cos\theta$.
* **Area Vector:** A vector that is always $90^\circ$ (perpendicular) to the surface itself. 
* **Orientation Matters:**
  * $0^\circ$: Max outward flux ($+EA$).
  * $90^\circ$: Zero flux ($0$).
  * $180^\circ$: Max inward flux ($-EA$).
* **Integration:** For curved surfaces, flux is the integral of the field over tiny area pieces: $\Phi = \int \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A}$.
* **SI Unit:** Newton-meters squared per Coulomb ($N \cdot m^2 / C$).
