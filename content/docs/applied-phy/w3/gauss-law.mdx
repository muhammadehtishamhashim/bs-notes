# Statement of Gauss's Law

Gauss's Law relates the electric flux through a boundary to the charge inside it. 

**The Law States:** The total amount of flux passing through an enclosed surface is equal to $1/\epsilon_0$ times the total charge ($q$) enclosed by that surface.

<MathWrapper title="Gauss's Law">

$$
\Phi = \frac{1}{\epsilon_0} \times q
$$

</MathWrapper>

---

# Proof of Gauss's Law

If we have a closed surface, let's assume it is a sphere containing a point charge $q$.

1. We know the flux integral is: $\Phi = \oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A}$.
2. For a sphere, the Area Vector and Electric Field are parallel, so $\cos(0^\circ) = 1$. The limits of integration for the area are from $0$ to the full surface area of a sphere ($2\pi r^2$, wait, a full sphere is $4\pi r^2$, adjusting to correct mathematical standard while maintaining logic).

<MathWrapper title="Deriving Gauss's Law">

$$
\begin{aligned}
\Phi &= E \int d\vec{A} \\[1em]
\Phi &= E \cdot [A] \quad \text{(from 0 to } 4\pi r^2) \\[1em]
\Phi &= E (4\pi r^2 - 0)
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

Now, we substitute the standard formula for the electric field ($E = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q}{r^2}$):

<MathWrapper title="Substituting Electric Field">

$$
\begin{aligned}
\Phi &= \left( \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q}{r^2} \right) (4\pi r^2) \\[1em]
\Phi &= \frac{1}{\cancel{4\pi}\epsilon_0 \cancel{r^2}} \times q \times (\cancel{4\pi} \cancel{r^2}) \\[1em]
\Phi &= \frac{1}{\epsilon_0} q \quad \text{(Hence Proved)}
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

# Differential Form of Gauss's Law

To find what happens at a microscopic point rather than a macro-surface, we derive the differential form. We start by comparing our known flux equations.

### Step 1: Comparing Equations
<MathWrapper title="Equation Comparison">

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{1. } \Phi &= \frac{1}{\epsilon_0} q \\[1em]
\text{2. } \Phi &= \vec{E} \cdot \vec{A} \\[1em]
\text{3. } \Phi &= \int_S \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} \\[1em]
\text{Comparing (1) and (3):} \\[1em]
\int_S \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} &= \frac{1}{\epsilon_0} q \quad \longrightarrow \text{(Equation 4)}
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

### Step 2: Volume Charge Density
Now, we use volume charge density ($\rho = \frac{dq}{dV}$). By integrating, we find the total charge: $q = \int_V \rho \, dV$. We substitute this new $q$ into Equation 4.

<MathWrapper title="Substituting Density">

$$
\text{New equation:} \quad \int_S \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{1}{\epsilon_0} \int_V \rho \, dV
$$

</MathWrapper>

### Step 3: The Divergence Theorem
Here we introduce a critical rule of calculus called the **Divergence Theorem**. It states that a surface integral over a closed boundary is equal to the volume integral of the divergence inside it.

$$\int_S \vec{V} \cdot d\vec{A} = \int_V (\nabla \cdot \vec{V}) dV$$

By applying the Divergence Theorem to our Electric Field ($\vec{E}$), we can rewrite the left side of our equation:

<MathWrapper title="Applying Divergence Theorem">

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{By using D.T we have:} \quad \int_S \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} &= \int_V (\nabla \cdot \vec{E}) dV \\[1em]
\text{So, put this into our equation:} \\[1em]
\int_V (\nabla \cdot \vec{E}) dV &= \frac{1}{\epsilon_0} \int_V \rho \, dV
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

Since both sides are volume integrals, the inside contents must be equal:

<Callout title="Differential Form" type="info">
$$\nabla \cdot \vec{E} = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0}$$
*(This can also be written as: $\text{Div } \vec{E} = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0}$)*
</Callout>

# Integral Form of Gauss's Law

Interestingly, we can easily derive the Integral Form by working backwards from the Differential calculations we just completed.

From our previous calculations, we know:
$$\int_V (\nabla \cdot \vec{E}) dV = \int_V \frac{1}{\epsilon_0} \rho \, dV$$

Using the **Divergence Theorem** in reverse, we convert the volume integral on the left back into a surface integral:

<MathWrapper title="Reversing via Divergence">

$$
\int_S \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = \int_V \frac{1}{\epsilon_0} \rho \, dV
$$

</MathWrapper>

We pull the constant ($1/\epsilon_0$) out of the right side. We also know that the integral of density over volume equals total charge ($q = \int \rho \, dV$). 

<MathWrapper title="Final Integral Form Derivation">

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_S \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} &= \frac{1}{\epsilon_0} \left( \int \rho \, dV \right) \\[1em]
\int_S \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} &= \frac{1}{\epsilon_0} q
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

<Callout title="Integral Form" type="info">
$$\oint_S \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{q}{\epsilon_0}$$
</Callout>

## Comprehensive Summary

* **Gauss's Law:** $\Phi = q/\epsilon_0$. The total flux through an enclosed surface depends solely on the charge inside it.
* **Proof:** Derived mathematically by integrating $E$ over a spherical area ($4\pi r^2$).
* **Differential Derivation:** 1. Equate flux integral to $q/\epsilon_0$.
  2. Substitute $q$ with volume integral of charge density ($\rho$).
  3. Apply the **Divergence Theorem** to convert the surface integral to a volume integral.
  4. Compare both sides to isolate the differential form: $\nabla \cdot \vec{E} = \rho/\epsilon_0$.
* **Integral Derivation:** 1. Start from the differential volume equality.
  2. Use the Divergence Theorem to convert the left side back to a surface integral ($\int_S \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A}$).
  3. Replace the volume integral of density on the right side with total charge ($q$). Resulting in the Integral form.
