# Core Properties of Electric Charge

Based on the fundamental behavior of subatomic particles, electric charge follows two major universal rules.

## 1. Conservation of Charge

**The Principle:** The net charge of an isolated system remains constant over time. 

Charge can neither be created nor destroyed; it can only be transferred from one object to another. 
* **Example**: When you rub a glass rod with silk, the glass loses electrons (becomes positive) and the silk gains the exact same amount of electrons (becomes negative). The total charge of the rod-silk system remains zero.
* **Energy connection**: Just as energy is conserved in a closed system, charge is strictly conserved. The energy required to move these charges around relates to electrostatic potential energy, but the sheer *amount* of charge never changes.

## 2. Quantization of Charge

**The Principle:** Electric charge is always quantized. It exists only in discrete packets, never in continuous amounts.

Any observable charge ($Q$) in the universe must be an integer multiple of the elementary charge ($e$). You cannot have half an electron.

$$Q = \pm ne$$

Where:
* $Q$ = Total charge
* $n$ = Integer ($1, 2, 3, ...$)
* $e$ = Elementary charge ($1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$)

**Application of Quantization:**
If a body has a charge of $3.2 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$, we can find how many electrons were removed:
$$n = \frac{Q}{e} = \frac{3.2 \times 10^{-19}}{1.6 \times 10^{-19}} = 2 \text{ electrons}$$
