# Permittivity of Free Space ($\epsilon_0$)

In Coulomb's Law ($F = k \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}$), the constant $k$ is known as Coulomb's constant. Its value in a vacuum is $9 \times 10^9 \text{ Nm}^2/\text{C}^2$.

We can also express $k$ in terms of the **permittivity of free space ($\epsilon_0$)**:
<MathWrapper title="Coulomb's Constant">

$$
k = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}
$$

</MathWrapper>

Where $\epsilon_0$ has a constant value of:
$$ \epsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12} \text{ C}^2/\text{Nm}^2 $$

### What is Permittivity?
Permittivity ($\epsilon$) measures a material's resistance to the formation of an electric field within it. 
* If $\epsilon \uparrow$ (increases), it means higher resistance ($R \uparrow$) to the field lines.
* Therefore, the force between charges decreases ($F \downarrow$).

# Relative Permittivity (Dielectric Constant)

If the charges are placed in a medium other than a vacuum (like water, glass, or air), the force between them changes because the medium resists the electric field differently.

* **Force in Vacuum ($F_v$)**:
  $$F_v = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}$$
* **Force in Medium ($F_m$)**:
  $$F_m = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_m} \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}$$

Here, $\epsilon_m$ is the permittivity of the specific medium. We can define **Relative Permittivity** (also called the Dielectric Constant, $\epsilon_r$):
<MathWrapper title="Relative Permittivity">

$$
\epsilon_r = \frac{\epsilon_m}{\epsilon_0} \implies \epsilon_m = \epsilon_r \epsilon_0
$$

</MathWrapper>

### Comparing Forces
If we divide $F_m$ by $F_v$, we get a very useful comparison formula showing how much the force is reduced:
<MathWrapper title="Force Reduction in a Medium">

$$
\frac{F_m}{F_v} = \frac{1}{\epsilon_r} \\[1em]
F_m = \frac{F_v}{\epsilon_r}
$$

</MathWrapper>

<Callout title="Example Calculation" type="info">
If the force between two charges in a vacuum is $F_v = 500 \text{ N}$, and they are moved into a medium with a dielectric constant $\epsilon_r = 25$:
$$F_m = \frac{500}{25} = 20 \text{ N}$$
*(The force is heavily reduced because the medium resists the electric field).*
</Callout>

# Vector Form of Coulomb's Law

Force is a vector quantity, meaning it has both magnitude and a specific direction.

<MathWrapper title="Vector Form of Coulomb's Law">

$$
\vec{F}_v = \left( \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \right) \hat{r}
$$

</MathWrapper>
*(Where $\hat{r}$ is the unit vector indicating direction along the line connecting the charges)*

**Newton's Third Law in Electrostatics:**
If charge 1 exerts a force on charge 2 ($F_{12}$), then charge 2 exerts an equal and opposite force on charge 1 ($F_{21}$).
$$F_{12} = -F_{21}$$
