# Calculating Charge on an Object

When dealing with macroscopic objects (like rods, sheets, or spheres), counting individual electrons is practically impossible. Instead, we assume the charge is evenly distributed and use **Charge Density**, which tells us how much charge is squeezed into a specific amount of space.

## 1-Dimensional: Linear Charge Density ($\lambda$)

For very thin, long objects like a wire or a rod, the charge is spread out along its total length ($L$).
We define linear charge density ($\lambda$) as the charge per unit length:
$$\lambda = \frac{dq}{dL}$$

To find the total charge ($q$) on the entire object, we can look at an infinitesimally small length element ($dL$) which holds a tiny amount of charge ($dq$):
$$dq = \lambda \cdot dL$$

We then integrate over the entire length of the rod (from $0$ to $L$):
<MathWrapper title="Total Charge on a 1D Object">
  $$ \int dq = \int_{0}^{L} \lambda \cdot dL $$
  $$ q = \lambda [L]_{0}^{L} $$
  $$ q = \lambda L $$
</MathWrapper>

<Callout title="Example: The 100-meter Rod" type="info">
If we have a rod of length $L = 100 \text{ m}$ with a linear charge density of $\lambda = 20 \text{ C/m}$, the total charge is:
$$q = 20 \times 100 = 2000 \text{ C}$$
</Callout>

## 2-Dimensional: Surface Charge Density ($\sigma$)

For flat, 2D continuous objects like a sheet of paper or a metal plate, charge is distributed evenly across its surface Area ($A$). We define surface charge density ($\sigma$) as the charge per unit area:
$$\sigma = \frac{dq}{dA}$$

Using integration to find the general formula:
<MathWrapper title="Total Charge on a 2D Object">
  $$ \int dq = \sigma \int dA $$
  $$ q = \sigma A $$
</MathWrapper>

---

## 3-Dimensional: Volume Charge Density ($\rho$)

For solid 3D objects like a solid sphere or a block, charge is distributed throughout its entire Volume ($V$). We define volume charge density ($\rho$) as the charge per unit volume:
$$\rho = \frac{dq}{dV}$$

Using integration to find the general formula:
<MathWrapper title="Total Charge on a 3D Object">
  $$ \int dq = \rho \int dV $$
  $$ q = \rho V $$
</MathWrapper>
