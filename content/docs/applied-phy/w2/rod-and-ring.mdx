## Electric Field due to a Line of Charge (Rod)

Imagine an infinitely long rod with an evenly distributed positive charge. We want to find the electric field ($E$) at a point $P$ at a distance $r$ from the rod. 

Using linear charge density ($\lambda = dq/dL$), we can write the electric field for a tiny segment of the rod:
<MathWrapper title="Electric Field of a Line Element">

$$
dE = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{dq}{r^2} \\[1em]
E = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \int \frac{\lambda \cdot dL}{r^2}
$$

</MathWrapper>

## Electric Field due to a Ring of Charge

A ring of charge is essentially the 1D line of charge (rod) bent into a circle. The physics remains one-dimensional, but we use a small arc length ($dS$) instead of a straight line segment ($dL$).

### Resolving Components
If we pick a point $P$ along the central axis of the ring, the electric field vector $dE$ from a tiny segment of the ring hits point $P$ at an angle $\theta$. We must break this into components:
* **X-axis (Horizontal)**: $dE_x = dE \cos\theta$
* **Y-axis (Vertical)**: $dE_y = dE \sin\theta$

<Callout title="The Cancellation Effect" type="warn">
For every segment on the top of the ring pushing *down* ($dE_y$ pointing down), there is an exact opposite segment on the bottom pushing *up* ($dE_y$ pointing up). Therefore, **all Y-components cancel each other out.** Only the X-components ($dE_x$) add up!
</Callout>

### Mathematical Derivation

Let's derive the exact formula for the total electric field at point $P$.

#### Step 1: Defining the Initial Equation
Since the Y-components cancel, the net electric field is purely the sum of all X-components ($dE_x$). We know that $dq = \lambda \cdot dS$.

<MathWrapper title="Setting up dEx">

$$
\begin{aligned}
dE &= dE_x = dE \cos\theta \\[1em]
dE &= \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{dq}{r^2} \\[1em]
dE &= \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{\lambda \cdot dS}{r^2} \\[1em]
\text{So now, } dE_x &= \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{\lambda \cdot dS}{r^2} \cos\theta
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

#### Step 2: Geometric Substitutions
Looking at the geometry of the ring, we can use the Pythagorean theorem where the hypotenuse is $r$, the base is distance $x$, and the perpendicular is radius $R$. We will substitute $r$ and $\cos\theta$ in our equation.

<MathWrapper title="Substituting Geometry Variables">

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{We know: } r^2 &= R^2 + x^2 \implies r = \sqrt{R^2 + x^2} \\[1em]
\text{And: } \cos\theta &= \frac{\text{Base}}{\text{Hypotenuse}} = \frac{x}{r} \\[1.5em]
dE_x &= \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{\lambda \cdot dS}{(R^2 + x^2)} \cos\theta \\[1em]
dE_x &= \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{\lambda \cdot dS}{(R^2 + x^2)} \left( \frac{x}{r} \right) \\[1em]
dE_x &= \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{\lambda \cdot dS}{(R^2 + x^2)} \frac{x}{(R^2 + x^2)^{1/2}} \\[1em]
dE_x &= \frac{x \cdot \lambda \cdot dS}{4\pi\epsilon_0 (R^2 + x^2)^{3/2}}
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

#### Step 3: Integrating over the Ring
Now we integrate to sum up all the tiny $dS$ segments around the entire ring. Because $x$, $\lambda$, and $R$ are constants for a specific point $P$, we can pull them out of the integral. The limits of integration for the arc length $dS$ are from $0$ to the full circumference $2\pi R$.

<MathWrapper title="Integration Step">

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int dE_x &= \int_{0}^{2\pi R} \frac{x \cdot \lambda \cdot dS}{4\pi\epsilon_0 (R^2 + x^2)^{3/2}} \\[1em]
E_x &= \frac{x \cdot \lambda}{4\pi\epsilon_0 (R^2 + x^2)^{3/2}} \int_{0}^{2\pi R} dS \\[1em]
E_x &= \frac{x \cdot \lambda}{4\pi\epsilon_0 (R^2 + x^2)^{3/2}} [S]_0^{2\pi R} \\[1em]
E_x &= \frac{x \cdot \lambda}{4\pi\epsilon_0 (R^2 + x^2)^{3/2}} (2\pi R) \\[1.5em]
\text{Since total charge } q &= \lambda (2\pi R): \\[1em]
E &= \frac{x \cdot q}{4\pi\epsilon_0 (R^2 + x^2)^{3/2}}
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

#### Step 4: Special Case (Point Far Away)
What happens if the radius $R$ is so small compared to the distance $x$ ($R \ll x$)? For example, $R = 1\text{m}$ and $x = 1000\text{m}$. 

We can assume $R \approx 0$ in our formula. Let's see how the formula simplifies:

<MathWrapper title="Special Case: R is negligible">

$$
\begin{aligned}
E &= \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{x \cdot q}{((0) + x^2)^{3/2}} \\[1em]
E &= \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{x \cdot q}{(x^2)^{3/2}} \\[1em]
E &= \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{x \cdot q}{x^3} \\[1em]
E &= \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q}{x^2}
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

<Callout title="Insight" type="info">
Notice that the final result $E = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q}{x^2}$ is the exact formula for a point charge! This means that if you go far enough away from a charged ring, the electric field behaves as if all the charge is concentrated into a single central point.
</Callout>

### Electric Field due to a Disc of Charge

While a ring is 1D (a bent wire), a disc is a solid 2D surface. To calculate the electric field of a solid disc, we use **Surface Charge Density ($\sigma$)**.

Instead of integrating tiny points, we calculate the electric field of the disc by thinking of it as a series of concentric *rings*, starting from a radius of $0$ all the way to the outer edge radius $R$. We sum (integrate) the electric fields of all these infinite rings together.

*(Note: The exact derivation involves integrating the ring formula from $0$ to $R$, resulting in a formula heavily dependent on surface charge density $\sigma$.)*
