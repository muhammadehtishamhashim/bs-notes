# Electric Field due to a Line of Charge (Rod)

Imagine an infinitely long rod with an evenly distributed positive charge. We want to find the electric field ($E$) at a point $P$ at a distance $r$ from the rod. 

Using linear charge density ($\lambda = dq/dL$), we can write the electric field for a tiny segment of the rod:
<MathWrapper title="Electric Field of a Line Element">
  $$ dE = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{dq}{r^2} $$
  $$ E = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \int \frac{\lambda \cdot dL}{r^2} $$
</MathWrapper>

# Electric Field due to a Ring of Charge

A ring of charge is essentially the 1D line of charge (rod) bent into a circle. The physics remains one-dimensional, but we use a small arc length ($dS$) instead of a straight line segment ($dL$).

## Resolving Components
If we pick a point $P$ along the central axis of the ring, the electric field vector $dE$ from a tiny segment of the ring hits point $P$ at an angle $\theta$. We must break this into components:
* **X-axis (Horizontal)**: $dE_x = dE \cos\theta$
* **Y-axis (Vertical)**: $dE_y = dE \sin\theta$

<Callout title="The Cancellation Effect" type="warn">
For every segment on the top of the ring pushing *down* ($dE_y$ pointing down), there is an exact opposite segment on the bottom pushing *up* ($dE_y$ pointing up). Therefore, **all Y-components cancel each other out.** Only the X-components ($dE_x$) add up!
</Callout>

## The Formula
Since $dE = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{dq}{r^2}$, and $dq = \lambda \cdot dS$:

<MathWrapper title="Total Electric Field of a Ring (X-axis)">
  $$ \int dE_x = \int \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{\lambda \cdot dS}{r^2} \cos\theta $$
</MathWrapper>

By integrating around the entire ring, we sum up all the $dS$ segments.

# Electric Field due to a Disc of Charge

While a ring is 1D (a bent wire), a disc is a solid 2D surface. To calculate the electric field of a solid disc, we use **Surface Charge Density ($\sigma$)**.

Instead of integrating tiny points, we calculate the electric field of the disc by thinking of it as a series of concentric *rings*, starting from a radius of $0$ all the way to the outer edge radius $R$. We sum (integrate) the electric fields of all these infinite rings together.

*(Note: The exact derivation involves integrating the ring formula from $0$ to $R$, resulting in a formula heavily dependent on surface charge density $\sigma$.)*
