# Electric Field due to a Point Charge

An electric field ($E$) is a region of space around a charged particle where a force would be exerted on other charged particles. It essentially describes the "electric pull or push" in the space surrounding a charge. The formula for the electric field produced by a point charge $q$ at a distance $r$ is:

<MathWrapper title="Electric Field Equation">

$$
E = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q}{r^2}
$$

</MathWrapper>

* $E$ is a vector quantity, measured in Newtons per Coulomb (N/C). It points away from positive charges and towards negative charges.

## Point Charge IN an Electric Field
If you take a test charge ($q_0$) and drop it into an existing uniform electric field ($E$), it will experience a force based on the strength of that field:
<MathWrapper title="Force on a Test Charge">

$$
F = q_0 E
$$

</MathWrapper>

Because $F = ma$ (Newton's Second Law), this electric force causes the charge to accelerate. The acceleration of the particle is:
$$a = \frac{q_0 E}{m}$$

# Electric Lines of Force

Electric field lines are a fantastic visualization tool to understand the strength and direction of the electric field visually.
1. They **originate** from positive charges and **terminate** on negative charges.
2. The tangent to a field line at any point gives the **direction** of the electric field $\vec{E}$ at that point.
3. Field lines **never intersect** each other.
4. The **density** of the lines represents the **strength** of the field (closer lines = stronger field, spread out = weaker field).

# Electric Dipole

An electric dipole consists of two equal and opposite point charges ($+q$ and $-q$) separated by a very small distance ($d$). They are very common in nature, such as in water molecules ($H_2O$).

* **Dipole Moment ($p$)**: A vector quantity pointing from the negative charge to the positive charge. It measures the strength of the dipole.
  $$p = q \cdot d$$

## Dipole in a Uniform Electric Field
When a dipole is placed inside an external electric field, the $+q$ charge is pushed in the direction of the field, and the $-q$ charge is pulled opposite to the field. 

This creates a rotational **Torque ($\tau$)** that tries to twist and align the dipole with the field lines:

<MathWrapper title="Torque on an Electric Dipole">

$$
\tau = p \times E \\
\tau = pE \sin\theta
$$

</MathWrapper>
*(Where $\theta$ is the angle between the dipole moment and the electric field).*
