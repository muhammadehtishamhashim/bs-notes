# Electric Field due to a Point Charge

An electric field ($E$) describes the "electric pull or push" in the space surrounding a charge. The electric field produced by a point charge $q$ at a distance $r$ is:

<MathWrapper title="Electric Field Equation">

$$
E = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q}{r^2}
$$

</MathWrapper>

* **Vector Quantity:** Measured in Newtons per Coulomb (N/C).
* **Direction:** Points *away* from positive charges and *towards* negative charges.

## Point Charge IN an Electric Field
If a test charge ($q_0$) is dropped into an existing uniform electric field ($E$), it experiences a force: $F = q_0 E$. 
Because $F = ma$, this force causes the charge to accelerate: $a = \frac{q_0 E}{m}$.

# Electric Lines of Force

Field lines are visual tools to map the electric field's strength and direction.
* **Origin & Termination:** Start on positive charges, end on negative charges.
* **Direction:** The tangent to a line indicates the field's direction at that exact point.
* **No Intersections:** Field lines never cross each other.
* **Strength:** Closer lines indicate a stronger field; spaced-out lines indicate a weaker field.

# Electric Dipole & Torque

An **electric dipole** consists of two equal and opposite point charges ($+q$ and $-q$) separated by a small distance ($d$).

* **Dipole Moment ($\vec{p}$):** A vector pointing from $-q$ to $+q$ measuring the dipole's strength. $p = q \cdot d$

## Torque in a Uniform Field
When placed in a uniform electric field ($\vec{E}$), the $+q$ is pushed with the field and $-q$ is pulled against it. This creates a rotational force called **Torque ($\vec{\tau}$)**.

<MathWrapper title="Torque on an Electric Dipole">

$$
\begin{aligned}
\vec{\tau} &= \vec{p} \times \vec{E} \\[1em]
\tau &= pE \sin\theta
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>
*(Where $\theta$ is the angle between the dipole moment $\vec{p}$ and the electric field $\vec{E}$).*

# Work and Potential Energy

To understand the energy stored in a dipole, we must map linear motion principles to angular motion.

* **Linear Work:** $dW = F \cdot dr$ (Force $\times$ linear displacement)
* **Angular Work:** $dW = \tau \cdot d\theta$ (Torque $\times$ angular displacement)

## Deriving Work Done on a Dipole
To find the total work ($W$) done in rotating a dipole from an initial angle $\theta_i$ to a final angle $\theta_f$, we integrate the angular work equation:

<MathWrapper title="Integration of Work">

$$
\begin{aligned}
W &= \int_{\theta_i}^{\theta_f} \tau \, d\theta \\[1em]
W &= \int_{\theta_i}^{\theta_f} pE \sin\theta \, d\theta \\[1em]
W &= pE \Big[ -\cos\theta \Big]_{\theta_i}^{\theta_f} \\[1em]
W &= -pE (\cos\theta_f - \cos\theta_i)
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

## Potential Energy ($U$)
The work done on the dipole is stored as **Potential Energy ($U$)**. 
$$U = -pE (\cos\theta_f - \cos\theta_i)$$

To find the absolute potential energy at any angle $\theta$, physicists assign a zero-energy reference point. **Assume $\theta_i = 90^\circ$** because the potential energy is zero when the dipole is perpendicular to the field.

<MathWrapper title="General Potential Energy Formula">

$$
\begin{aligned}
U &= -pE (\cos\theta_f - \cos 90^\circ) \\[1em]
U &= -pE (\cos\theta - 0) \\[1em]
U &= -pE \cos\theta \implies U = -\vec{p} \cdot \vec{E}
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

# 3 Special Cases of Potential Energy

Using the formula $U = -pE \cos\theta$, we can analyze the dipole's energy state based on its alignment with the electric field.

### Case 1: $\theta = 0^\circ$ (Parallel)
<MathWrapper title="Minimum Energy">

$$
\begin{aligned}
U &= -pE \cos(0^\circ) \\
U &= -pE(1) \\
U &= -pE \quad \text{(Minimum Energy)}
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>
<Callout title="Stable Equilibrium" type="info">
When perfectly aligned with the field, the dipole has minimum potential energy. This is a state of **stable equilibrium** (it doesn't want to move).
</Callout>

### Case 2: $\theta = 90^\circ$ (Perpendicular)
<MathWrapper title="Zero Energy">

$$
\begin{aligned}
U &= -pE \cos(90^\circ) \\
U &= 0 \quad \text{(Zero Energy)}
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>

### Case 3: $\theta = 180^\circ$ (Anti-Parallel)
<MathWrapper title="Maximum Energy">

$$
\begin{aligned}
U &= -pE \cos(180^\circ) \\
U &= -pE(-1) \\
U &= +pE \quad \text{(Maximum Energy)}
\end{aligned}
$$

</MathWrapper>
<Callout title="Unstable Equilibrium" type="warn">
When perfectly against the field, the dipole has maximum potential energy. This is a state of **unstable equilibrium** (the slightest nudge will cause it to violently spin to $0^\circ$).
</Callout>

## Comprehensive Summary

* **Electric Field:** Calculates the force per unit charge ($E = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q}{r^2}$). Point charges dropped in a field will accelerate ($a = \frac{q_0E}{m}$).
* **Field Lines:** Visual arrows pointing from positive to negative. Density dictates field strength.
* **Dipole & Torque:** Two opposite charges separated by $d$ create a dipole moment $p=qd$. In a uniform field, forces cause it to twist (Torque: $\tau = pE\sin\theta$).
* **Angular Work:** Work done rotating the dipole is the integral of torque ($W = \int \tau d\theta$).
* **Potential Energy:** The energy stored during rotation is $U = -pE\cos\theta$ (assuming $U=0$ at $90^\circ$).
* **Equilibrium States:** * $0^\circ$: Stable, Minimum Energy ($-pE$)
  * $90^\circ$: Zero Energy ($0$)
  * $180^\circ$: Unstable, Maximum Energy ($+pE$)
